PART 01
Amazon Does Everything Wrong

In 2011, a senior Google engineer named Steve Yegge wrote an internal rant that accidentally went public. He'd spent six and a half years at Amazon before joining Google, and he opened with a striking claim: Amazon does everything wrong, and Google does everything right.

"There are probably a hundred or even two hundred different ways you can compare the two companies, and Google is superior in all but three of them."

— STEVE YEGGE, GOOGLE ENGINEER (2011)

His list of Amazon's failures was long. Their hiring process was inconsistent because each team hired for itself. Their engineers spent so much time on operations they barely had time to code. Their offices were bare-bones. Their pay was mediocre. Their code was a mess with no engineering standards. He described the codebase as "a bunch of crappy tools that read and write state machine information into relational databases."

Google, by contrast, had better hiring, better tools, better perks, better code, better everything. By almost every measure Yegge could think of, Google was the superior company.

So why would anyone write a rant about this? Because Yegge believed Amazon had one thing that made up for all of it. One thing Google was completely missing. And that one thing, he argued, mattered more than everything else combined.

PART 02
But Then Bezos Did Something

Around 2002, Amazon's CEO Jeff Bezos issued what Yegge called the most important mandate in the company's history. Bezos was known for micro-managing every pixel on Amazon's website and handing out sticky notes reminding people "who runs the company." But this particular mandate was different. It wasn't about pixels or products. It was about how teams work together.

Bezos told every team in the company to follow six rules. In plain language, the rules said:

1. Every team must make its work available to other teams through a clear, structured interface — like a menu that others can order from.

2. If you need something from another team, you go through that menu. No shortcuts.

3. No backdoors, no informal workarounds, no "just ask Dave to pull the data." The menu is the only way.

4. Teams can build their menu however they want. Bezos didn't care about the technical details.

5. Every menu must be designed as if outside developers — people who don't work at Amazon — could use it someday.

6. Anyone who doesn't follow these rules will be fired.

— BEZOS MANDATE, PARAPHRASED (CIRCA 2002)

Rule #5 was the crucial one. Bezos wasn't just reorganizing his company. He was forcing every team to build as if the outside world might eventually plug into their work. At the time, Amazon was an online store. Nobody would have guessed it needed to be an "extensible, programmable platform."

But Bezos had realized two things. First, all the infrastructure Amazon built for selling books could be repurposed as a computing platform anyone could rent. That became Amazon Web Services (AWS). Second, he realized he couldn't always build the right product for every customer. He needed to let third parties build on top of Amazon's capabilities.

PART 03
The Golden Rule: Eat Your Own Dogfood

Yegge then introduced what he called the Golden Rule of Platforms: "Eat Your Own Dogfood." This means: if you're building a platform for others, you should be forced to use that same platform yourself. Don't give yourself special shortcuts or secret backdoors that external developers don't get. If the platform is painful for outsiders, it should be painful for you too — because that's how you'll be motivated to fix it.

He rephrased it even more directly: "Start with a platform, and then use it for everything." You can't build a great product first and bolt on a platform later. That order matters.

"Microsoft has known about the Dogfood rule for at least twenty years. It's been part of their culture for a whole generation now. You don't eat People Food and give your developers Dog Food."

— YEGGE ON MICROSOFT

Yegge acknowledged that Microsoft wasn't great at design, but they had over thirty years of experience building platforms. Their developer portal (MSDN) had thousands upon thousands of tools and resources. Apple understood the power of third-party development through the App Store. Facebook understood it by letting outside developers build Farmville, Mafia Wars, and hundreds of other apps — making Facebook a different experience for every user.

And Google? Yegge described Google's developer portal as looking like "what your fifth-grade nephew might mock up if he were doing an assignment to demonstrate what a big powerful platform company might be building if all they had, resource-wise, was one fifth grader."

PART 04
Why Google+ Was Doomed

Google+ launched in 2011 as Google's answer to Facebook. It had some clever features — Circles for grouping friends, Hangouts for video chat. As a product, it was arguably well-designed. But Yegge argued that this was exactly the problem: Google thought Facebook won because it built a great product. That diagnosis was wrong.

"Facebook is successful because they built an entire constellation of products by allowing other people to do the work. So Facebook is different for everyone."

— YEGGE ON WHY FACEBOOK WON

Google's response to seeing Facebook's ecosystem of third-party games and apps was to go contract someone to write games for Google+. Do you see the difference? Facebook created a platform and let thousands of developers figure out what users want. Google tried to predict what users want and deliver it themselves.

"There have been precious few people in the world, over the entire history of computing, who have been able to reliably predict what people want. Steve Jobs was one of them. We don't have a Steve Jobs here. I'm sorry, but we don't."

— YEGGE, BEING BLUNT

The lesson: unless you're a once-in-a-generation product visionary, you can't out-product a platform. Google+ had features. Facebook had an ecosystem. Features can be copied. Ecosystems create network effects that are self-reinforcing.

PART 05
The Closing Argument

Yegge's entire rant built toward a single, forceful thesis:

"A product is useless without a platform, or more precisely and accurately, a platform-less product will always be replaced by an equivalent platform-ized product."

— YEGGE'S CENTRAL THESIS

He argued that Google was a "Product Company through and through" — built on the wild success of Search, which biased everyone toward thinking they could keep predicting what users want and delivering it perfectly. But Bezos, despite being a control freak, realized something Yegge felt Google's leadership hadn't: you don't need to be Steve Jobs if you build a platform that lets others solve the problem for you.

His closing was urgent. Google needed a cultural transformation. Not just a few teams thinking about APIs, but an "all-hands-on-deck emergency." He acknowledged it might not be too late, "but the longer we wait, the closer we get to being Too Late."

The rant was meant to be internal. Yegge accidentally posted it publicly, it went viral, and it became one of the most widely read essays on platform strategy ever written — by an engineer, not a business school professor.
